PMCUProperty Mgmt of Chulalongkorn U.สำนักงานจัดการทรัพย์สิน จุฬาฯ
1939

Chulalongkorn University receives ownership of the land.

1975

The Property Management Office of Chulalongkorn University (PMCU) is established to administer the estate.

1987

Samyan Market is renovated.

2005

The Chamchuri Square project (begun 1994 as CU-Hi-Tech Square) opens.

2008

The new Samyan Market opens.

2011

Construction of CU iHouse and CU Terrace.

2012

The CU Centenary Park project is launched.

2015

The “Dragon Town” (Zy Walk) project.

2017

Initiated the Chula Smart City project — the master plan that reframes each block as land to be redeveloped.

2019

Samyan Mitrtown opens.

2021

Development of the Block 33 project begins.

S01Scala Theatreโรงหนังสกาลา
1969

Scala opens as part of the “Apex Group” alongside the Siam and Lido; its first screening is The Undefeated — Art Deco, a massive bronze chandelier.

1997

Through the 1970s–2000s, as other standalone theatres close, Scala survives on film festivals, indie films and old-school charm — ushers in yellow blazers.

2010

The Siam Theatre fire (19 May, amid political unrest) collapses the historic cinema; Scala becomes Bangkok's last standalone cinema, the final symbol of independent movie houses in Thailand.

2020

Covid-19 and the lease expiration: Scala screens its final film, Cinema Paradiso, and the building returns to PMCU.

2021

Scala is demolished for new shopping-mall development — heritage law protects only 100-year-old structures, leaving the modern icon unprotected.

S10Suan Luang Squareสวนหลวงสแควร์
1960

Mid-20th century: a hub of shophouses — Suanluang Market, the Siang Kong district and sports-equipment shops, a grassroots circular economy.

2012

PMCU's “Suanluang–Samyan Master Plan” (2013–14): aggressive rent hikes and short-term leases push out the shopkeepers who built the neighborhood.

2015

Block 47's identity is erased for “Suanluang Square” — the name kept for branding while residents are evicted; a lifestyle mall for high-income consumers.

2019

“Dragon Town” and the Suanluang Square flea market boost the lifestyle image; high-profit daily rentals to outside vendors manufacture vibrancy while erasing stable local livelihoods.

S05Chao Mae Thapthim Shrineศาลเจ้าแม่ทับทิม
1850

The Thap Thim shrine is established (1850s–1870s) by Teochew Chinese migrants in the Saphan Luang area.

1960

A fire destroys the shrine's original wooden structure; it is soon rebuilt — a sign of the community's resilience.

2008

After the 2008 lease terminations the shrine enters a period of uncertainty as the surrounding community is cleared.

2010

As Samyan Mitrtown plans progress, the university proposes a new shrine within the redeveloped site — relocation framed as preservation.

2015

During Samyan Mitrtown construction, tensions emerge between community, caretakers and the university; a new shrine is prepared at the project's edge.

2020

On 27 May 2020 PMCU issues an official notice asking the caretaker to vacate the site at Lane 33.

Now

The shrine becomes a site of collective resistance — flower vendors, incense sellers and motorcycle taxis intertwining everyday economy with cultural preservation.

S04Samyan Mitrtownสามย่านมิตรทาวน์
1850

Small communities — Chinese migrants and local residents — settle the area, engaging in trade, agriculture and small-scale commerce.

2008

Chulalongkorn University begins ending leases under a phased redevelopment plan, displacing residents and dissolving the community.

2010

Plans for Samyan Mitrtown progress; relocation and reconstruction are framed as a way to preserve the shrine despite loss of context.

2015

Most of the previous community is displaced; informal street life and small-scale economy largely disappear during construction.

2019

Mitrtown opens to a new user base; community life shifts from place-based living to transient, consumption-driven activity.

S07Former Chinese Church (7th-day Adventist)อดีตโบสถ์จีนเซเว่นเดย์
1987

Construction of the current church building is completed — a neighborhood landmark for over 50 years.

2020

Community erasure accelerates: land reclamation for the Smart City and rising rents force long-term residents and businesses out.

Now

Demolition of the church — a vernacular sacred space erased while institutional monuments are protected. The surviving trees remain as living monuments to those who once lived here.

S02Two King Monument Squareพระบรมราชานุสาวรีย์สองรัชกาล
1987

The monument honors King Chulalongkorn and King Vajiravudh on the university's 70th anniversary; it hosts freshman oaths and graduations — institutional sacred space.

2017

A professor assaults student leader Netiwit Chotiphatphaisal during a freshman walk-out, sparking national debate. The square is ceremonial ground more than public space — little shade, unusable for daily life.

S06CU iHouse (former Suan Luang School)เรือนวิรัชมิตร
1932

Established as Suan Luang School, educating the children of university staff and the surrounding community.

2011

After the land transfers to Chulalongkorn, the school is demolished; CU iHouse (international student housing) and CU Terrace are built in its place.

S08Faculty of Comm. Arts & Educationคณะนิเทศศาสตร์ และ คณะครุศาสตร์
1969

The Faculty of Communication Arts (founded 1965, formerly part of Political Science) builds out on land absorbed from the surrounding community; the Faculty of Education grows alongside it.

2011

Successive faculty buildings (1969 · 1974 · 1999 · 2011) extend the campus over the former neighborhood.

S03Chamchuri Squareจามจุรีสแควร์
1994

Starts as the joint venture “CU-Hi-Tech Square” — meant to be Thailand's biggest IT hub. The Tom Yum Goong crisis hits; money runs out and it stands as a ‘ghost tower’ for nearly a decade.

1997

Chula takes the project over, lowers the residential height and adapts the plan to the new economy.

2005

Opens as “Chamchuri Square” — a mix of offices, mall and residential.

S09CU Centenary Parkอุทยาน 100 ปี จุฬาฯ
2008

The Suan Luang–Samyan area: a vibrant neighborhood — the ‘Sieng Kong’ auto-parts community and legendary food stalls — the last land community of the corridor.

2012

Thousands of residents are evicted to make way for the park.

2017

For Chula's 100th anniversary the university launches a new Master Plan, providing green space for the urban area.

2019

Opens as an award-winning ‘Urban Sponge’ — 29 rai retaining millions of gallons of floodwater, while the displaced community is gone (PR markets sustainability over the people erased).
